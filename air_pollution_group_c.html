National Environment Policy 2018 seeks environmental conservation, pollution control, biodiversity protection, and climate resilience as part of sustainable development. The Group-C task is to convert those broad goals into implementable air pollution control actions.

This deck follows the attached six-step exercise: strategic review, strategy drafting, resource requirements, M&E loop, policy revision, and legal alignment.

STEP 1

Strategic points in NEP 2018

Existing policy narratives

  • • Pollution prevention over post-damage response.
  • • Environmental considerations in development planning.
  • • Sector coordination among ministries and agencies.
  • • Public health and ecosystem protection as policy outcomes.
  • • Technology, awareness, and compliance-based enforcement.

Strategic gaps to address

  • • Weak source-wise targets for PM2.5 and seasonal smog.
  • • Limited city-level clean air action plans.
  • • Fragmented monitoring and weak emissions inventories.
  • • Inadequate enforcement against non-compliant kilns and vehicles.
  • • Low integration of health evidence into policy review.
STEP 2

Sector strategy statements

Brick kilns

Accelerate conversion to cleaner kiln technologies, enforce siting restrictions, phase out fixed chimney kilns, and tie licensing to emissions compliance.

Transport

Tighten vehicle fitness checks, fuel quality standards, traffic management, and public transport upgrades; prioritize high-emission fleet replacement.

Industry

Require stack monitoring, cleaner fuels, and pollution control devices in major industrial clusters; link permits to real compliance data.

Construction and roads

Mandate dust suppression, covered transport of materials, roadside sweeping, and demolition waste management in urban authorities.

Municipal waste

Ban open burning, strengthen segregation and collection, and use sanitary disposal systems to reduce toxic local emissions.

Public health response

Integrate AQI alerts, hospital preparedness, school guidance, and seasonal risk communication during winter pollution peaks.

STEP 5

Policy revision and dynamic adaptation

Revision triggers

Revise targets if AQI trends worsen, if new scientific evidence emerges, or if existing controls fail in peak winter months.

Adaptive tools

Seasonal clean-air protocols, emergency school-health advisories, and hotspot-based enforcement campaigns.

Learning mechanism

Use research pilots, health surveillance, and local government feedback to refine policy every 2–3 years.

Strategic adaptation means the policy remains goal-stable but instrument-flexible: standards stay firm while enforcement sequencing, incentives, and local action plans evolve with evidence.
